Basic Rules

Magic is essentially a card duel between two mages. You win by reducing your opponent's life total to 0 life (mostly through combat damage from creatures), although some cards can enable you to win in other ways.

Quick summary of what you need to know before your first game

  • Putting cards into play costs mana. Mana usually comes from lands.
  • You can normally play one land per turn. The number of spells you can play isn't limited (but mana sources usually are).
  • Creatures attack and block. Unblocked attackers damage the opponent.
  • You play most spells during your turn. Except instants, which can be played at any time (even during your opponent's turn).
  • To "tap" means to rotate the card. Tapping is shown with the tap symbol .
  • Tip: Always read the card's type line first. It tells you the most about a card at a glance.

How to read a card

1] Card Name and Mana Cost

  • Card name is in the top-left bar.
  • Mana cost is in the top-right corner of that same bar.
  • The mana cost also shows the card’s colors at a glance: W (white), U (blue), B (black), R (red), G (green).
  • A number in a circle indicates generic mana (can be paid with any color). If the mana cost has only numbers or colorless symbols (no colors), the card is usually colorless.

2] Artwork window

The large picture box under the top bar is the artwork. It is essential for establishing card's characteristics, environment, and tone.

3] Type line and Set symbol

  • The type line is the thin strip directly under the art (e.g., Creature — Elf Warrior).
  • The set / expansion symbol sits on the right side of that same strip.
  • The symbol’s color shows rarity: black = common, silver = uncommon, gold = rare, copper red = mythic rare.

4] Rules text and Flavor text

  • The big text box below the type line contains the rules text (basically what the card does).
  • If present, flavor text is below rules text in the same box, italicized, to serve as card's backstory, lore and narrative.

5] Power/Toughness (creatures only)

Creature cards have a small box in the bottom-right corner showing Power/Toughness like 4/3 on the example card. Noncreature cards won’t have this.

6] Bottom edge (fine print row)

Along the bottom edge you’ll often find small-print metadata like collector's number, artist credit, as well as additional card/set details and copyright text.

Casual ElderForge Game Setup

  • Sort basic lands, i.e. Plains, Islands, Swamps, Mountains and Forests into 5 respective piles face-up.
  • Sort all regular cards into 6 respective piles based on their color, i.e. White, Blue, Black, Red, Green and Multicolor+Colorless pile, shuffled, face-down.
  • Sort all extra mythic cards into a saparate, shuffled, face-down pile.
  • Before the duel, each player rolls 6 color dice to determine which colors they're playing.

  • Each player shuffles their final deck (called library in-game) and draws 5 cards.
  • Decide who goes first (roll a die, coin flip, whoever lost last time, etc.).

Anatomy of a turn

  1. Untap — Untap your tapped cards.
  2. Upkeep — When “During your upkeep” abilities happen.
  3. Draw — Draw a card (either from basic land piles or your library).
  4. 1st Main phase — Play a land, cast spells, activate abilities.
  5. Combat — Declare attackers, then opponent declares blockers, then damage is dealt.
  6. 2nd Main phase — Cast more spells after combat if you want.
  7. End step — When End-step abilities happen; damage wears off; discard down to 5.

Mana and casting spells

  • To cast a spell, pay its mana cost (top-right of the card).
  • You usually pay by tapping lands for mana; lands untap at the start of your next turn.
  • Unused mana empties when a turn ends.

Card types (quick model)

  • Lands — Permanent cards. Main mana source. Played only on your turn, usually one per turn.
  • Creatures — Permanent cards. They attack/block, deal combat damage, and have special abilities.
  • Instants — Fast spells. Cast them at almost any time (often in response to your opponent's spells).
  • Sorceries — Slow spells. Cast only during your main phases when the stack is empty.
  • Enchantments / Artifacts — Permanent cards that provide ongoing effects.

Combat (how damage happens)

  • You choose which creatures attack.
  • Opponent chooses blockers and assigns them to attackers.
  • Unblocked attackers deal damage to the opponent.
  • Blocked creatures deal damage to the creatures that blocking them, and vice versa. Some creatures may die in the process.
  • Most creatures have summoning sickness: they can’t attack or use tap abilities the turn they enter play unless they have haste.

Zones (where certain cards are placed)

During a game, cards move between a few standard areas called zones. Knowing these makes the table layout feel obvious instead of chaotic.

Zone What it is
Hand Cards you’ve drawn but haven’t played yet. Your hand is private unless an effect says otherwise.
Library Your face-down deck. You draw from the top. If an effect tells you to search your library, you always shuffle afterward (unless the effect explicitly says not to).
Play Cards “in play” are so-called permanents that sit on the table in front of you: lands, creatures, artifacts, enchantments, etc. Most ongoing effects come from cards in play.
Graveyard Your discard pile. Destroyed creatures go here. Instants and sorceries usually go here after they resolve. The graveyard is public, meaning your opponent can look at it whenever they want.
Exile A separate public zone for cards removed from the game by “exile” effects. Exile is different than graveyard — it’s usually much harder to get cards back from exile.
Stack This is not a physical pile, but it still counts as a zone. When a spell is cast or an ability is activated, it doesn't resolve immediately, but instead goes on the so-called stack. Players can then respond with a spell or ability of their own. If they do, that goes on top of the stack, and so on. When no one wants to respond, the stack finally resolves last-in, first-out. Instants and sorceries usually go to the graveyard after resolving. Permanents usually resolve by entering play.

Quick table layout tip

  • Keep lands in a row closest to you, then creatures above them.
  • Keep your graveyard and exile separate so you never mix them up.
  • Keep your library in a consistent corner and always draw from the top.

One last note

You don’t need to memorize everything. If you know the turn structure, mana, and combat, you can play — the rest is more or less just learning what each individual cards do, which is the real fun part of this game.
