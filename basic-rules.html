Basic Rules

Magic is essentially a duel between two mages. You win by reducing your opponent to 0 life (usually through combat damage from creatures), though some cards can win in other ways.

What you need to know before your first game

  • Cards cost mana. Mana usually comes from lands.
  • You normally play one land per turn.
  • Creatures attack and block. Unblocked attackers damage the opponent.
  • Most spells happen on your turn. Instants are the fast ones.
  • “Tap” means rotate the card. Tapping is shown with the tap symbol .
  • Always read the type line. It tells you what the card is.

How to read a card by where your eyes go

Read a Magic card like a dashboard: identity up top, picture for vibe, rules for behavior, then combat stats and metadata.

1) Top bar: Name (left) and Mana Cost (right)

  • Card name is in the top-left bar.
  • Mana cost is in the top-right corner of that same bar.
  • The mana cost also shows the card’s colors at a glance: W (white), U (blue), B (black), R (red), G (green).
  • A number in a circle is generic mana (paid with any color). If the cost has only numbers / colorless symbols and no colored symbols, the card is usually colorless.

2) Artwork window (upper half)

The large picture box under the top bar is the artwork. It’s not rules text, but it often hints at creature type, environment, and tone.

3) Type line + Set symbol (strip under the art)

  • The type line is the thin strip directly under the art (e.g., Creature — Elf Warrior).
  • The set / expansion symbol usually sits on the right side of that same strip.
  • On most modern frames, the symbol’s color shows rarity: black = common, silver = uncommon, gold = rare, orange/copper = mythic rare.

4) Rules text (and flavor text)

  • The big text box below the type line contains the rules text (what the card does).
  • If present, flavor text is usually below rules text in the same box, often italicized and separated by spacing.

5) Power/Toughness (creatures only)

Creature cards have a small box in the bottom-right corner showing Power/Toughness like 3/2. Noncreature cards won’t have this.

6) Bottom edge (fine print row)

Along the bottom edge you’ll often find small-print metadata like collector number, artist credit, and legal/copyright text.

Setup

  • Each player shuffles their deck (library) and draws 5 cards.
  • Decide who goes first (roll a die, coin flip, etc.).

Anatomy of a turn

  1. Untap — Untap your tapped cards.
  2. Upkeep — “During your upkeep” abilities happen.
  3. Draw — Draw a card (either from basic land piles or your library).
  4. 1st Main phase — Play a land, cast spells, activate abilities.
  5. Combat — Declare attackers, then blockers, then damage.
  6. 2nd Main phase — Cast more spells after combat.
  7. End step — End-step abilities happen; damage wears off; discard down to 5.

Mana and casting spells

  • To cast a spell, pay its mana cost (top-right of the card).
  • You usually pay by tapping lands for mana; lands untap at the start of your next turn.
  • Unused mana empties as steps/phases end (unless a card says otherwise).

Card types (quick model)

  • Lands — Main mana source. Played only on your turn, usually one per turn.
  • Creatures — Stay in play. They attack/block. P/T is bottom-right (e.g., 3/2).
  • Instants — Fast spells. Cast them at almost any time.
  • Sorceries — Slower spells. Cast only during your main phase when the stack is empty.
  • Enchantments / Artifacts — Permanents that stay in play and provide ongoing effects.

Combat (how damage happens)

  • You choose which creatures attack.
  • Opponent chooses blockers.
  • Unblocked attackers deal damage to the opponent.
  • Blocked creatures deal damage to the creatures blocking / being blocked.
  • Most creatures have summoning sickness: they can’t attack or use tap abilities the turn they enter play unless they have haste.

Stack and timing (the “response” concept)

When a spell is cast or an ability is activated, it goes on the stack. Players can respond. The stack resolves last-in, first-out.

Zones (where cards live)

During a game, cards move between a few standard areas called zones. Knowing these makes the table layout feel obvious instead of chaotic.

Hand

Cards you’ve drawn but haven’t played yet. Your hand is private unless an effect says otherwise.

Library

Your face-down deck. You draw from the top. If an effect tells you to search your library, you shuffle afterward (unless the effect explicitly says not to).

Play

Cards “in play” sit on the table in front of you: lands, creatures, artifacts, enchantments, etc. Most ongoing effects come from cards in play.

Graveyard

Your discard pile. Destroyed creatures go here. Instants and sorceries usually go here after they resolve. The graveyard is public information.

Exile

A separate public zone for cards removed from the game by “exile” effects. Exile is not the graveyard — it’s usually harder to get cards back from exile unless a card specifically allows it.

Stack

This is not a physical pile, but it’s a real zone: when you cast a spell or activate an ability, it goes on the stack first. Players can respond. Then it resolves last-in, first-out. Instants and sorceries usually go to the graveyard after resolving. Permanents resolve by entering play.

Quick table layout tip

  • Keep lands in a row closest to you, creatures above them.
  • Keep your graveyard and exile separate so you never mix them up.
  • Keep your library in a consistent corner and always draw from the top.

One last note

You don’t need to memorize everything. If you know turn structure, mana, and combat, you can play — the rest is learning what your cards do.
