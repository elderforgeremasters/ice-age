Ice Age Lore

Dominaria is an Earthlike world—spherical, with broad continents and oceans under a familiar sun—and it has two moons: the natural Mist Moon and the artificial Null Moon. In the Ice Age, that sun often feels muted behind heavy cloud and endless snowfall, so daylight turns pale and grudging, as if the world is rationing warmth.

During the Shard of Twelve Worlds era, people still see the moons rise and the seasons inch along, but many swear the sky feels wrong: planar travel is choked off and the world’s metaphysics feel “sealed,” which makes strange omens plausible and persuasive—auroras sharper than they should be, constellations that seem oddly fixed in certain regions, and cult whispers that the Null Moon is watching rather than merely orbiting.

They called it the Ice Age — the Time of Ice, the Time of the Eternal Snows. A winter so long it stopped being weather and became history. Terisiare didn’t merely grow colder. It was reshaped, renamed, and rerouted by ice until the continent itself felt like a maze built to test survival.

1) The world starts freezing

The cold had been creeping in for centuries, but the true turning point came after the Sylex Blast that ended the Brothers’ War. The blast scarred Dominaria’s mana and climate, and the decline accelerated. Within a few generations, the Ice Age is considered fully begun: institutions collapse, borders harden, and the snow stops behaving like something temporary.

Glaciers advance like empires. The Great Glacier fills the Lat-Nam Straits, binding old coastlines into new shapes while sealing regions behind sheer walls of ice.

Places are abandoned not because they lose wars, but because the land itself becomes an enemy.

2) Terisiare is remade by ice

As the freeze deepens, Terisiare becomes a new continent wearing old bones. Ronom Glacier expands south until even the long-abandoned Great Desert is effectively erased and remembered as the Northern Wastes. Lakes stop flowing. Valleys become traps. A place that once gave life becomes a place that steals it — Ronom Lake turns into the deadly swamp called the Frost Marsh.

Whole societies adjust. Old city-states fade. New peoples harden into nations: the Kjeldorans build for discipline and endurance; the Balduvians survive through fierce mobility and will. In the east, the forests fed by glacial waters defy the freeze — and the seed of what becomes Yavimaya proves the world still remembers how to grow.

3) The hidden refuge of magic

Civilization doesn’t stop — it goes underground, behind wards, into secrecy. When the City of Shadows is abandoned, scholars flee and eventually establish the School of the Unseen: a hidden campus and sanctuary for spellcasters, built to endure when ordinary cities cannot.

Elsewhere, ambition finds a different form. On the ruins of old Kroog, the city of Soldev rises — a place where invention and obsession share the same workshop. In the Ice Age, “progress” is never clean.

4) The Shard of Twelve Worlds

Then the world gets smaller. As a side-effect of the Sylex Blast, the Multiverse around Dominaria is wrapped into an envelope called the Shard of Twelve Worlds. Travel into or out of this pocket becomes impossible or severely limited.

Some planeswalkers are trapped within the Shard. Others are locked out. Terisiare is now a stage with the doors bolted shut. The ancient players who are already inside can linger… and meddle.

5) Storgard falls, Kjeldor rises

Centuries pass. The Ice Age grinds on. And then comes one of its defining tragedies: the Fall of Storgard. The Raynor Glacier presses down upon the city, and the corruption of King Miko by Tevesh Szat twists leadership when clarity is most needed.

Storgard fractures over a single question: stay, or flee? The decision is put to a duel between Jason Carthalion and Freyalise (before her ascension). Jason wins — and those who choose to leave are spared the fate of the city.

The refugees settle upon the ruins of old Penregon and found Kjeld. What begins as flight becomes a nation: Kjeldor. Those who remain in Storgard are eventually swallowed by the glacier and erased. The Ice Age doesn’t just kill — it removes the evidence.

6) The late Ice Age tightens: secrets, proxies, and a final convergence

By the end of the era, Terisiare is a continent full of hardened nations and refined magic — but also a place where planeswalkers still cast long shadows. The Shard remains. The cold remains. And conflicts increasingly feel like proxy wars: mortals fighting while larger hands steer the pieces.

Planeswalkers gather at the Summit of the Null Moon to discuss breaking the Shard. But the meeting is poisoned by private agendas. Death strikes: the Elder Dragon Chromium Rhuell dies, and Faralyn’s spellsquire Ravidel is killed and resurrected — a kindness that drives him into fury and madness. The summit fails to end the Shard, but it plants the final idea that will.

7) Lim-Dûl’s Battle: the Last Battlefield

In that same late span, Terisiare erupts into its final great Ice Age war. The necromancer Lim-Dûl brings an undead host through the Karplusan passes, and the defenders gather at the mouth of a mountain route north of Krov.

Lovisa Coldeyes leads Balduvia. General Varchild leads Kjeldor. Mages fight openly: Jodah the archmage, Jaya Ballard the taskmage, and others whose names become welded to the era’s ending. The battle is savage, uneven, and personal — with deaths that echo through the stories that follow.

At the climax, Jodah duels Lim-Dûl. Lim-Dûl calls on Leshrac for aid — and Leshrac arrives, not to save him, but to express disappointment. Leshrac severs Lim-Dûl’s hand, breaks his connection to Mairsil’s ring, and planeswalks away with him. The Ice Age’s final battle ends not with a neat victory, but with a cruel removal.

The site becomes known as the Last Battlefield — the last war fought during the Ice Age.

8) The World Spell: ending the Ice Age

Immediately after, Freyalise acts. She casts the World Spell to break the Shard and end the Ice Age. The ritual uses the Ice Cauldron (a replica of the Sylex) and Jodah’s mirror, used to “show the land its true self.” Kaysa is present, as are Jodah and Jaya, Kolbjörn and others.

The spell’s power is on par with the Sylex Blast — potentially devastating. The energy is dispersed across Terisiare via safe havens, which are destroyed in the process. The world survives the cure. The cure leaves scars. And the ice finally loosens its grip.

9) The Thaw and the Flood Age

The next era is called The Thaw, but it begins with a betrayal of expectations: warmth doesn’t arrive as peace. It arrives as water.

As the Great Glacier, Ronom Glacier, Raynor Glacier, and the Boreal ice retreat, meltwater reshapes the continent. The Sea of Laments forms, drowning regions and splitting Terisiare into the Terisian Isles. Lands that rose when the sea fell are reclaimed; peoples are displaced again. Some survivors flee toward Yavimaya and make hard bargains with the living world there.

The Thaw is not epilogue — it’s the second catastrophe. It creates new nations in fertile ground, while swallowing old ones under rising seas.

10) Alliances: when buried machines wake

Not long into the Thaw, the melting ice also reveals what should have stayed buried. In Soldev, the Soldevi Adnates pry open locked vaults. Arcum Dagsson commands wonders — but wonders are not always loyal. Excavated Phyrexian War Beasts are activated, and Soldevi Steam Beasts are turned into weapons.

Soldev is destroyed in a storm of machinery and magic. In the aftermath, the catastrophe spills into the School of the Unseen, drawn toward a deep source of black mana — a bottomless pit tied to darker forces. The School, long a refuge, is shattered. Survivors scatter. Jodah fades into myth. Jaya’s power erupts and changes her forever.

11) Coldsnap: winter’s last, organized bite

Even after the World Spell, the cold refuses to die politely. Movements rise that want the world frozen again. The Cult of Rimewind becomes a force — not a place, but an ideology armed with undead, orcs, Stromgald influence, and whatever war-machines the melt coughs up.

Kjeldor fights again in the early Thaw, not merely against enemies on the field, but against the idea that the past can be revived if enough people fear the future. The Ice Age ends — and then has one more argument about it.

That’s the record: a world frozen by ancient disaster, sealed by planar consequence, hardened into nations, shaken by necromancy and machines, cracked open by a spell powerful enough to end an era — and then tested again by those who refused to let winter go.
